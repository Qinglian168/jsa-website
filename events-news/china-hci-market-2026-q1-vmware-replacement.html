3VMware Replacement: The Driving Force

Broadcom's acquisition of VMware and the subsequent elimination of perpetual licenses have triggered what industry observers call the largest infrastructure migration wave in China's enterprise IT history. Tens of thousands of organizations that built their data centers on vSphere, vSAN, and NSX now face a stark choice: pay dramatically higher subscription fees, or migrate to alternative platforms.

Why Enterprises Are Replacing VMware in 2026:

  • License cost explosion — Broadcom ended perpetual licensing; subscription costs have increased 3-5x for many enterprise customers, making renewal financially untenable
  • Xinchuang (信创) compliance mandate — Government, finance, and critical infrastructure sectors face accelerating domestic technology replacement deadlines, favoring native Chinese HCI platforms
  • Architecture modernization — The migration window presents an opportunity to adopt container-native, AI-ready infrastructure rather than simply replicating legacy virtualization
  • Operational continuity — Leading vendors like Sangfor provide 236 PowerCLI-compatible commands, enabling near-zero learning curve for VMware-trained operations teams

Sangfor's approach is particularly noteworthy for its 100% feature parity with VMware: compute virtualization (matching vSphere with 97%+ CPU performance conversion), network virtualization (matching NSX with sub-10μs latency), storage virtualization (matching vSAN with enhanced virtual shared disks), intelligent O&M (matching vRealize), and disaster recovery (matching SRM with RPO as low as 1 second — outperforming VMware's native offering).

Case Study: Guojin Securities

As an AA-rated securities firm, Guojin Securities deployed 23+ HCI clusters across two cities and five data centers using Kunpeng ARM and Hygon C86 architectures. Core trading systems were migrated from VMware with RTO < 5 minutes and RPO < 30 seconds, achieving financial-grade high availability with zero business interruption.

For enterprises still on VMware, the window for orderly migration is narrowing. JSA Solution recommends beginning the assessment process immediately — evaluating workload inventory, identifying migration candidates, and conducting POC testing with at least two domestic HCI platforms before committing to a vendor.

4Software-Defined Cost Optimization: Doing More with Less

The second major trend identified by IDC is the industry-wide shift from “hardware stacking” to “software efficiency”. With hardware costs climbing across CPU, memory, and storage components, enterprises are leveraging software-defined capabilities to maximize resource utilization, repurpose existing assets, and reduce dependency on new hardware procurement.

Two Key Software-Defined Cost Optimization Strategies:

1. Resource Reuse (资源利旧)

A end-to-end process: inventory assessment → idle resource identification → legacy hardware refurbishment → cluster deployment. Enterprises can deploy new business workloads on existing hardware without large-scale new purchases.

Case: A Shenzhen power manufacturing enterprise consolidated 500+ idle VMs, releasing 1,000+ CPU cores and 2TB of idle memory, dramatically reducing new hardware procurement while ensuring smart systems went live on schedule.

2. Memory Tiering Technology (内存分层技术)

Sangfor's innovative multi-level memory pool + intelligent scheduling engine uses NVMe SSD to expand memory capacity. Under the same performance guarantees, this technology delivers 2x effective memory capacity from the same physical RAM.

Case: A Shanghai automotive software provider used Sangfor's “resource reuse + memory tiering” solution to repurpose 2 existing servers and purchase only 4 new ones. They achieved 768G effective memory from 384G physical RAM, saving 1 million RMB in procurement costs.

These case studies validate a critical insight: software-defined technology is the key to navigating hardware price inflation cycles. Enterprises that invest in software-defined infrastructure today — whether through HCI platforms, SDS solutions, or network virtualization — are building a foundation that can absorb cost shocks through intelligent resource management rather than brute-force hardware expansion.

5Full-Stack HCI: The New Competitive Frontier

IDC defines full-stack HCI as systems that go beyond traditional hyper-converged architecture by integrating platform-layer services tailored to specific business scenarios. These systems provide end-to-end abstraction across compute, storage, and network layers, offering enterprises a “one-stop” solution from underlying resources to upper-layer business applications— forming an “HCI infrastructure + platform services” collaborative model.

What is the difference between HCI software market and HCI overall market?

The HCI overall market includes both HCI appliances (integrated hardware) and HCI software integrated systems. The HCI software market specifically tracks independently sold HCI software and the hardware it drives, where the software and certified servers have a loosely coupled relationship. In Q1 2026, SmartX led the HCI software market with 36.8% share (13 consecutive quarters at #1), while Sangfor led the overall HCI market with 19.2% share. Enterprises prioritizing hardware flexibility and avoiding lock-in should consider software-focused vendors, while those seeking standardized one-stop procurement may prefer appliance vendors.
